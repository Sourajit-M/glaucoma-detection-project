\begin{abstract}\bfseries
Glaucoma is a leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide, yet it remains significantly underdiagnosed due to the requirement for specialist fundus image interpretation. This paper presents an interpretable hybrid deep learning system for automated glaucoma detection using retinal fundus images. The proposed approach combines transfer learning (ResNet18), structural optic disc and cup segmentation (U-Net), and gradient-weighted class activation mapping (Grad-CAM) for clinical explainability. Three publicly available datasets were used for evaluation: ACRIMA (705 images), RIM-ONE DL (485 images), and EyePACS-AIROGS-light-v2 (3,540 images), yielding a combined test set of 1,050 samples.

A systematic comparison was conducted between classical machine learning baselines (Logistic Regression, SVM-RBF, Random Forest) and deep learning architectures (ResNet18, EfficientNet-B0). An ablation study evaluated four fusion strategies combining CNN predictions with handcrafted features and U-Net-derived cup-to-disc ratio (CDR). The proposed CNN+CDR hybrid model achieved an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of 0.9468, sensitivity of 0.9300, and specificity of 0.8018 on the held-out test set, significantly outperforming the best classical ML baseline (SVM-RBF, AUC\,=\,0.789, $p < 0.05$, McNemar's test). The U-Net achieved Dice coefficients of 0.968 and 0.880 for optic disc and cup segmentation respectively on DRISHTI-GS1. Grad-CAM visualisations confirmed consistent model attention on the clinically relevant optic disc region, supporting clinical trustworthiness. The ablation study demonstrated that U-Net-derived CDR provides complementary structural information to the CNN (AUC gain: +0.002), while handcrafted SVM features are subsumed by the CNN's learned representations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Glaucoma affects approximately 80 million people globally and is the second leading cause of blindness after cataracts~\cite{tham2014}. Unlike other blinding conditions, glaucomatous vision loss is irreversible once it occurs; however, early detection and treatment can halt progression in the majority of cases~\cite{weinreb2004}. The primary clinical indicator of glaucoma is enlargement of the optic cup relative to the optic disc, quantified as the cup-to-disc ratio (CDR). A CDR exceeding 0.65 is generally considered indicative of glaucomatous structural damage~\cite{jonas1988}.

Automated analysis of retinal fundus photographs offers a scalable and cost-effective approach to population-level glaucoma screening. However, several challenges persist: glaucoma indicators are spatially localised to the optic disc region; global image classifiers introduce peripheral noise that can mislead predictions; and most existing deep learning models lack clinical interpretability, limiting clinician trust and adoption.

Existing approaches to automated glaucoma detection fall into three broad categories: (1) classical machine learning on handcrafted features~\cite{niemeijer2007}; (2) end-to-end deep convolutional neural networks~\cite{chen2015}; and (3) segmentation-based methods that explicitly extract structural features~\cite{orlando2020}. Few works integrate all three components into a unified, interpretable system, and most studies evaluate on a single dataset, limiting generalisability claims.

This paper makes four primary contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A systematic multi-dataset evaluation comparing classical ML and deep learning approaches to glaucoma detection across three heterogeneous datasets.
  \item A U-Net-based optic disc and cup segmentation pipeline achieving state-of-the-art Dice coefficients on DRISHTI-GS1, enabling reliable CDR extraction across datasets.
  \item A principled ablation study identifying CNN+CDR as the optimal fusion strategy, demonstrating that U-Net-derived structural features provide complementary information to deep visual features.
  \item Grad-CAM explainability analysis validating that the model attends to clinically relevant optic disc regions, supporting trustworthy deployment.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Classical Machine Learning Approaches}
Early automated glaucoma detection systems relied on handcrafted features including colour histograms, texture descriptors such as Local Binary Patterns (LBP)~\cite{ojala2002}, and geometric optic disc measurements. Niemeijer et al.~\cite{niemeijer2007} demonstrated that CDR extracted from manual segmentation, combined with a linear discriminant classifier, could achieve competitive screening performance. However, these methods require careful feature engineering and are sensitive to image quality variation across different fundus camera devices.

\subsection{Deep Learning for Glaucoma Detection}
Convolutional neural network (CNN)-based approaches have increasingly dominated the literature since 2015. Chen et al.~\cite{chen2015} demonstrated that fine-tuned AlexNet outperformed classical baselines on the ORIGA dataset. More recently, transformer-based architectures and attention mechanisms have shown further improvements~\cite{li2021}. However, most studies evaluate on a single proprietary dataset, raising questions about generalisability, and the majority do not provide clinical explainability.

\subsection{Segmentation-Based Methods}
The REFUGE Challenge~\cite{orlando2020} established U-Net variants as the leading approach for optic disc and cup segmentation, with top-performing methods achieving Dice coefficients exceeding 0.95 for disc and 0.85 for cup. CDR computed from predicted masks has been shown to correlate well with expert clinical measurement~\cite{fumero2011}, providing a structurally grounded complement to image-level classifiers.

\subsection{Explainability in Medical AI}
Selvaraju et al.~\cite{selvaraju2017} proposed Grad-CAM as a class-discriminative visualisation technique applicable to any CNN architecture. Its application to retinal disease detection has shown that models attending to clinically relevant regions generalise better and are more readily accepted by clinicians in prospective studies~\cite{jiang2019}.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Preprocessing Pipeline}
All fundus images underwent a standardised preprocessing pipeline prior to feature extraction or CNN training:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{CLAHE enhancement}: Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation was applied to the L channel in LAB colour space to enhance optic disc visibility.
  \item \textbf{Circular masking}: The fundus boundary was detected via Otsu thresholding and the largest contour was used to zero out non-retinal background.
  \item \textbf{Resizing}: Images were resized to $224\times224$ pixels using Lanczos interpolation for CNN inputs, and $256\times256$ for U-Net.
  \item \textbf{Normalisation}: Pixel values were normalised using ImageNet statistics.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{sample_images.jpg}
    \caption{\footnotesize Visualisation of the preprocessing pipeline across different datasets. The application of CLAHE and circular masking standardises the contrast and removes peripheral noise, enhancing the visibility of the optic disc.}
    \label{fig:preprocessing}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Handcrafted Feature Extraction}
A 45-dimensional feature vector was extracted per image for classical ML baselines, comprising three groups: Colour features (18-dim), LBP texture features (26-dim, green channel), and a CDR proxy feature (1-dim).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{lbp_visualisation.jpg}
    \caption{\footnotesize Local Binary Pattern (LBP) texture feature extraction on the green channel of a fundus image, demonstrating how classical ML models capture structural variations.}
    \label{fig:lbp_features}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Classical Machine Learning Models}
Three classical ML classifiers were evaluated using stratified 5-fold cross-validation with GridSearchCV on the training split: Logistic Regression, SVM with RBF kernel, and Random Forest. Models were trained on the combined train+validation split and evaluated on the held-out test split.

\subsection{Deep Learning Architecture}
A ResNet18 backbone pretrained on ImageNet was adapted for glaucoma classification. Mixed-precision training (\texttt{torch.cuda.amp}) was enabled throughout. EfficientNet-B0 was evaluated under identical conditions to ensure a fair architectural comparison.

\subsection{U-Net Segmentation}
A U-Net with a ResNet18 encoder was trained separately for optic disc and optic cup segmentation on DRISHTI-GS1. Due to the small training set (50 images), aggressive augmentation was applied. CDR was computed from predicted binary masks as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{CDR} = \sqrt{\frac{A_{\text{cup}}}{A_{\text{disc}}}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Proposed Hybrid Model: CNN + CDR Fusion}
A late-fusion ensemble was constructed using a Logistic Regression meta-learner trained on the stacked predictions of the CNN and U-Net CDR. 
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}_{\text{meta}} = [P_{\text{CNN}}(\text{glaucoma}),\; \text{CDR}_{\text{U-Net}}] \in \mathbb{R}^{2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Performance Analysis}
The proposed CNN+CDR hybrid achieves AUC 0.947, which compares favourably with recently published glaucoma detection systems. The +0.158 AUC improvement over SVM-RBF demonstrates that deep visual features substantially outperform handcrafted feature engineering on this task.

\subsection{Ablation Findings}
The negative impact of SVM feature fusion is an important finding. It suggests that colour and texture features are sufficiently captured by ResNet18's convolutional layers — adding them to the meta-learner creates collinear inputs that add noise rather than signal. 

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{gradcam_errors.jpg}
    \caption{\footnotesize Failure mode analysis using Grad-CAM. In certain false negative cases, the model's attention drifts towards peripheral artifacts or pathological anomalies unrelated to the optic disc.}

Limitations include the small size of the DRISHTI-GS1 dataset for U-Net training, potential cross-dataset distribution shifts, and the current limitation to binary classification rather than multi-class severity grading.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented an interpretable hybrid deep learning system for glaucoma detection from retinal fundus images. The proposed CNN+CDR model fuses ResNet18 deep visual features with U-Net-derived cup-to-disc ratio, achieving AUC of 0.947 and sensitivity of 0.930 on a 1,050-sample multi-dataset test set — a significant improvement over classical ML baselines (SVM-RBF, AUC\,=\,0.789, $p < 0.001$, McNemar's test). 

Future work will focus on training U-Net on larger annotated segmentation datasets, incorporating explicit domain adaptation for cross-dataset generalisation, and prospective clinical validation in a screening setting.
